Layerwise Causal Peeling in Linear Structural Equation Models

Explained: Feb 2026

Introduction

The central question this paper tackles is: Under what mathematical conditions can we uncover the true hidden causal variables purely from seeing the data ($\mathbf{x}$), without ever poking the system?

The Intuition Gap

Standard theory (like ICA) tells us that without extra information, there are infinite ways to interpret the data. This paper narrows that infinite space down to one unique solution (up to trivial changes) by imposing structure on the mechanism rather than requiring external interventions.

Problem Setup

We consider a linear structural equation model where the observed variables $X \in \mathbb{R}^d$ are generated according to:

$$ X = B X + \epsilon $$

where $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ is the weighted adjacency matrix of the causal graph, and $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma_\epsilon)$ is the noise vector with diagonal covariance. The key assumption is that the graph is acyclic (DAG), which implies there exists a topological ordering such that $B$ becomes strictly upper triangular.

The central question is: Can we recover the causal ordering and the matrix $B$ from i.i.d. samples of $X$?

Main Idea: Hessian-Based Peeling

The key insight is that leaf nodes (nodes with no children) in the causal DAG can be identified through their conditional independence structure. The paper proposes using the Frobenius norm of the Hessian of the conditional log-likelihood as a criterion:

$$ j^* = \arg\min_{j \in [d]} \| \nabla^2 \ell(X_j \mid X_{-j}) \|_F $$

where $\ell(X_j \mid X_{-j})$ is the conditional log-likelihood of variable $X_j$ given all other variables $X_{-j}$. Intuitively, a leaf node has minimal Hessian norm because it depends on its parents but doesn't influence any children.

Algorithm Overview

The algorithm proceeds in layers:

1. Initialization: Start with the full set of variables $\mathcal{V} = \{1, \ldots, d\}$.

2. Identify leaf layer: For each remaining variable $j \in \mathcal{V}$, compute:

$$ H_j = \nabla^2 \ell(X_j \mid X_{\mathcal{V} \setminus \{j\}}) $$

Select the variable(s) with minimal $\|H_j\|_F$ as the current layer.

3. Remove and repeat: Remove identified variables from $\mathcal{V}$ and repeat step 2 until all variables are ordered.

4. Recover weights: Once the ordering is established, estimate $B$ using least squares regression respecting the causal order.

Theoretical Guarantees

Under mild conditions on the noise distribution and the spectral properties of $B$, the paper shows that:

$$ \mathbb{P}\left( \text{correct ordering recovered} \right) \to 1 \quad \text{as} \quad n \to \infty $$

where $n$ is the number of samples. The key requirements are:

(a) The noise $\epsilon_i$ is sub-Gaussian with variance lower bounded by $\sigma^2_{\min} > 0$.

(b) The graph satisfies a "layer separation" condition, ensuring that leaf nodes are distinguishable from non-leaf nodes through their Hessian magnitudes.

(c) Sample complexity scales as $n = \Omega(d^2 \log d / \sigma^4_{\min})$ for consistent recovery.

Computational Complexity

Computing the Hessian for each variable requires $O(d^3)$ operations (matrix inversion). Since we need to evaluate this for all $d$ variables across $O(d)$ layers, the total complexity is $O(d^4)$ per iteration, with overall complexity $O(d^5)$.

For practical implementation, the authors suggest using approximate Hessian estimation via finite differences or autodifferentiation, reducing the constant factors.

Practical Considerations

Advantages: The method is parameter-free (no tuning required), handles equal variances, and provides exact recovery guarantees under the stated assumptions.

Limitations: The approach assumes linearity and Gaussian noise. Extensions to nonlinear SEMs or non-Gaussian noise distributions require additional structural assumptions (e.g., additive noise models).

Example: Three-Variable Chain

Consider the simple chain $X_1 \to X_2 \to X_3$:

$$ \begin{aligned} X_1 &= \epsilon_1 \\ X_2 &= b_{12} X_1 + \epsilon_2 \\ X_3 &= b_{23} X_2 + \epsilon_3 \end{aligned} $$

In the first iteration, we compute Hessians for all three variables. Since $X_3$ is a leaf, its Hessian norm $\|H_3\|_F$ will be smallest (it regresses only on its parent $X_2$, with no downstream effects). We identify $X_3$ first, remove it, then proceed to find $X_2$, and finally $X_1$.

Connection to Other Methods

This approach is related to but distinct from:

GES/PC algorithms: These use conditional independence tests but don't exploit the Hessian structure. Layerwise peeling provides stronger guarantees under the linear-Gaussian assumption.

Score-based methods (e.g., NOTEARS): These optimize over the space of DAGs directly. The peeling approach is more interpretable and doesn't require solving a challenging continuous optimization problem.

ICA-based methods (e.g., LiNGAM): These assume non-Gaussian noise to achieve identifiability. Layerwise peeling works under Gaussian noise but requires acyclicity and specific variance conditions.

Open Questions

Several directions remain open:

(i) Can the sample complexity be improved to $O(d \log d)$? Current bounds are quadratic in $d$.

(ii) How does the method perform under model misspecification (e.g., mild nonlinearities)?

(iii) Extensions to cyclic graphs or latent confounders?

Conclusion

The layerwise peeling algorithm provides an elegant and theoretically grounded approach to causal structure learning in linear SEMs. By exploiting the differential geometry of the likelihood surface (via Hessians), it achieves consistent recovery without requiring non-Gaussian assumptions or complex optimization procedures.
